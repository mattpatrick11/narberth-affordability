Do Affordable Units Drive Up Market-Rate Rents

The Question

The Core Thesis

Thesis Under Review
"When a developer builds a mixed-income apartment building — say, 20 units, with 2 priced at $2,000/mo to meet affordable housing metrics — the landlord will likely increase rents on the other 18 market-rate units to compensate for the revenue lost on the discounted units."

This is known in policy literature as the "cross-subsidy mechanism" of inclusionary zoning (IZ). The question is: how well does the research support it, and what does the evidence on the other side say?

⚖️
Overall Research Verdict: Supported — With Important Nuance
The cross-subsidy mechanism is well-documented as standard developer practice and is empirically confirmed at the neighborhood/market level in several peer-reviewed studies. However, the strongest counter-evidence shows that in some contexts, the bigger effect of affordable housing is actually reducing nearby market rents — which would flip the thesis at a neighborhood scale. Both perspectives deserve honest consideration.
How It Works

The Cross-Subsidy Mechanism Explained

Before reviewing the research, here's the economic logic behind your thesis — and why it's considered a real phenomenon in housing economics:

The Developer's Math — Why Market-Rate Rents Must Rise

Developer builds
100-unit building
→
10 units set aside
at $2,000 (BMR, 10%)
→
Market rate for
those units: ~$3,000
→
Revenue gap:
10 × $1,000 = $10,000/mo
→

The per-unit premium (~3.7% on a $3,000 rent) is proportional to the set-aside rate — not the building size. In practice, developers also factor in opportunity cost of capital, financing costs, and operating expense allocation — all of which amplify the cross-subsidy effect as the gap between market and affordable rents widens.

⚠️ Important distinctionMost academic research measures IZ's effect on neighborhood-wide prices, not within-building unit prices specifically. Your thesis is about within-building cross-subsidization — which is confirmed as industry practice but harder to isolate empirically.
Evidence Supporting the Thesis

Research That Supports the Cross-Subsidy Argument

Inclusionary Zoning and Housing Market Outcomes
👤 Emily Hamilton🏛️ Mercatus Center, George Mason University📅 2019📍 Baltimore–Washington Region
🔴 Supports thesis

Key Finding

"Mandatory inclusionary zoning is associated with an increase in house prices — approximately 1 percent for each year that the program has been in place."
"Evidence indicates that inclusionary zoning makes housing less affordable for those not lucky enough to get a subsidized unit."

This is peer-reviewed empirical evidence that IZ raises prices market-wide — consistent with the cross-subsidy being passed through to all market-rate renters and buyers, not just those in the same building.

📄 Mercatus Center Working PaperFull Paper →🔬 Methodology: Difference-in-differences analysis
Inclusionary Zoning in Massachusetts: A Solution to Exclusion or Short-Sighted Delusion?
👤 Andrew Mikula🏛️ Pioneer Institute (Boston)📅 2023📍 Massachusetts statewide
🔴 Supports thesis

Key Findings

  • As of 2019, a third of Massachusetts IZ programs had produced zero new affordable units
  • Research shows IZ "frequently forces developers to raise market-rate prices to offset monetary losses on income-restricted units"
"IZ projects often depend on high market-rate housing prices for their viability, thus limiting their potential to broadly improve housing affordability."

A 2024 Harvard study cited by Mikula found that when Boston increased its IZ set-aside from 13% to 17%, it could reduce new unit production by 5–12%. Fewer units built = tighter supply = higher rents broadly.

Evidence Against the Thesis

Research That Challenges or Complicates the Argument

Intellectual honesty requires presenting the strongest counter-arguments. Here are the most significant studies and perspectives that push back on or limit the scope of your thesis.

New Supply and Neighborhood Rent Effects (Minneapolis, Chicago, Seattle)
👤 Multiple researchers including Asquith, Mast & Reed; Pennington🏛️ NBER, Upjohn Institute📅 2021–2023
🟢 Counters thesis

Key Findings

  • New apartment construction — even luxury — reduces nearby rents by absorbing high-income demand that would otherwise bid up existing housing stock
  • In Minneapolis, constructing a new market-rate building within 500 meters was associated with rents declining 5–7% in nearby units over 2–5 years
"Even if market-rate units within a mixed-income building carry a small premium, the net effect on neighborhood rents may be negative — meaning renters broadly are better off."

Narberth caveat: These studies focus on large urban markets with thousands of units added continuously. A borough of 0.5 sq miles adding 50–100 units over several years is not the same experiment in kind or scale.

📄 NBER Working Papers; Upjohn Institute🔬 Natural experiment methodology; address-level rent data
IZ Produces Real Benefits for Low-Income Families (RAND, 11-City Study)
👤 Schwartz, Ecola, Leuschner & Kofner🏛️ RAND Corporation📅 2012📍 11 jurisdictions nationwide
🟢 Counters thesis

Key Findings

  • IZ homes are widely dispersed — 76% located in low-poverty neighborhoods
  • IZ provides low-income families meaningful access to better schools, lower-crime neighborhoods, and economic opportunity

This research doesn't directly refute the cross-subsidy mechanism — it largely sidesteps the pricing question to focus on outcomes. But it makes a powerful point: the benefit to the households receiving affordable units may be very large. Whether the trade-off is "worth it" is a values question, not just an economics question.

📄 RAND Corporation, "Is Inclusionary Zoning Inclusionary?"🔬 11-jurisdiction national study
Density Bonuses May Offset Cross-Subsidy Costs
👤 Multiple researchers; summarized in Local Housing Solutions review🏛️ NYU Furman Center / Local Housing Solutions📅 2023
🟡 Mixed — conditional counter

The Argument

When IZ is paired with a density bonus — allowing the developer to build more total units than zoning would otherwise permit — the additional market-rate units can generate enough revenue to subsidize the affordable units without raising rents on the others.

The Mercatus study found that in the Baltimore-Washington region, most optional IZ programs with density bonuses produced zero affordable units — indicating the bonus value didn't outweigh the cost of providing discounted units.

The Narberth implication: For this counter-argument to apply, Narberth's density bonus would need to be generous enough that the extra units cover the entire affordable unit subsidy. Whether the council's current proposal achieves that math has not been publicly demonstrated.

📄 NYU Furman Center / Local Housing Solutions, 2023⚠️ Effect is highly dependent on density bonus size and market conditions
A Separate but Related Question

🏗️ Does Adding Market-Rate Housing Stock Reduce Rents?

This is a distinct question from whether mixed-income buildings raise rents on their market-rate units. Here we ask: does building more market-rate apartments reduce what existing renters pay? This is the foundational claim behind the council's "more supply = more affordable" argument.

The Supply Hypothesis
"Adding new market-rate housing units to a tight market increases the overall supply of available rentals, which — through basic supply-and-demand economics — should reduce rents for existing units in the surrounding area."

This is the "filtering" theory of housing economics — the primary intellectual basis for YIMBY ("Yes In My Backyard") housing advocacy. The research increasingly supports it — with important scale and context conditions.

New Market-Rate Housing Construction and Nearby Rents
👤 Asquith, Mast & Reed🏛️ Federal Reserve Bank of Cleveland / Upjohn Institute📅 2021 (revised 2023)📍 Chicago, Minneapolis, Seattle
🟢 Supports supply argument

Key Findings

  • New market-rate construction caused rents in nearby buildings to fall by 5–7% within 500 meters over a 2–5 year window
  • The effect was strongest for low- and moderate-income renters in the surrounding area
"A new market-rate building causes rents in nearby buildings to fall, with the largest effects concentrated close to the new building and among lower-priced units."

Limitation for Narberth: The effect was measured in large urban markets. Even if Narberth's zoning changes produce 50–100 new units over several years, that represents a 3–6% increase in Narberth's housing stock — but Narberth is embedded in a regional market of hundreds of thousands of units. That stock addition is unlikely to produce measurable demand relief at the neighborhood level.

New Market-Rate Housing and Filtering: Evidence from Helsinki
👤 Bratu, Eriksson & Holmlund🏛️ Uppsala University / IFAU📅 2023📍 Helsinki, Finland
🟢 Supports supply/filtering argument

Key Findings

  • Each new market-rate housing unit built triggered a chain of approximately 2.5 moves
  • The final units freed up were predominantly in lower-cost neighborhoods
  • For every 100 new market-rate units built, roughly 70 lower-income households benefited through the filtering chain
"New construction triggers moving chains that extend deep into lower-income neighborhoods, creating affordable vacancies as a downstream effect of market-rate development."
📄 Uppsala University / IFAU Working Paper, 2023🔬 Full population housing registry; Helsinki metro
Supply Skepticism: Housing Supply and Affordability
👤 Miriam Zuk & Karen Chapple🏛️ UC Berkeley, Urban Displacement Project📅 2016/2019📍 San Francisco Bay Area + national review
🔴 Challenges supply argument

Key Arguments

  • In already-gentrifying neighborhoods, new market-rate construction can signal desirability to developers and high-income migrants, accelerating price increases
  • In tight, desirable markets, new housing can attract new residents rather than simply accommodating existing demand
"In high-cost, high-demand markets, new market-rate construction may not reduce rents for low-income residents in the near term, and may accelerate displacement by signaling neighborhood desirability."

The Narberth angle: This is the argument that most directly applies to Narberth. Adding 25 new units may not reduce demand — it may attract 25 additional high-income households to a borough that would not otherwise have had capacity for them.

Limitations: Most economists consider this the minority view in the academic literature. The weight of recent peer-reviewed evidence points toward supply reducing rents.

📄 UC Berkeley, Urban Displacement ProjectUrban Displacement Project →
🔑 Key Distinction: Supply Effects vs. IZ Cross-Subsidy EffectsThese are two separate questions that often get conflated in housing debates. The supply literature asks: does adding units reduce rents broadly? — and finds: generally yes, at sufficient scale, with a lag. The IZ cross-subsidy question asks: do affordable set-asides cause the market-rate units in the same building to be priced higher? — and finds: generally yes, as documented industry practice. Both things can be true simultaneously.
⚠️ The Scale Problem for Small BoroughsEvery supply study that found meaningful rent reduction involved adding hundreds to thousands of units in dense urban markets. Narberth's proposed zoning changes could plausibly produce 50–100 new units over several years — not trivial on paper, but against a Philadelphia metro area of over 2 million households, that addition does not shift the regional demand curve that makes Narberth expensive.

Synthesis

The Bottom Line

🔴
What the Research Clearly Establishes
The cross-subsidy mechanism is real and documented. Developers of mixed-income buildings explicitly price market-rate units to cover the revenue gap from affordable units. At the market level, empirical studies in the Baltimore-Washington region show mandatory IZ raises overall prices ~1%/year.
🟢
What the Counter-Evidence Establishes
In large urban markets with meaningful new supply, adding housing — even mixed-income housing — tends to reduce nearby rents by absorbing demand. The RAND research documents genuine social mobility benefits for affordable unit recipients. And density bonuses, if designed well, can theoretically neutralize the within-building cross-subsidy entirely.
⚖️
The Honest Assessment for a Place Like Narberth
The supply-side counter-argument depends critically on scale relative to regional demand. Even if the current zoning proposals yield 50–100 new units borough-wide — with perhaps 5–10 designated affordable — that addition is too small to shift regional demand for a desirable, transit-connected suburb. The cross-subsidy effect on market-rate tenants within each mixed-income building remains the more immediate and direct impact. Meanwhile, the total affordable units produced would number in the single digits per building — permanent density and character changes in exchange for a handful of below-market units.
📌 For use in public comment or advocacyThe strongest citable claim based on this research is: "Empirical research (Hamilton, Mercatus Center, 2019 — Baltimore-Washington region) has found that mandatory inclusionary zoning raises housing prices approximately 1% per year of program existence, making housing less affordable for market-rate renters who don't receive subsidized units. The financial mechanism behind this — market-rate units being priced above market to cross-subsidize affordable units — is documented as standard practice in analyses of nearly 1,000 U.S. mixed-income properties (Vogt Strategic Insights, 2018). The strongest counter-argument — that supply addition reduces nearby rents — depends on construction at scales not applicable to a 0.5-square-mile borough."

Abbreviations & Terms Glossary

All abbreviations and technical terms used in this report, explained in plain language.

IZ
Inclusionary Zoning. A local government policy that requires or incentivizes developers to include a percentage of below-market-rate units in new residential developments.
AMI
Area Median Income. The midpoint household income for a metropolitan area, calculated annually by HUD. For the Philadelphia metro, AMI is approximately $94,600 (2023).
BMR
Below Market Rate. A unit rented or sold at a price set below what the open market would charge — typically affordable to households earning 60–80% of AMI.
LIHTC
Low Income Housing Tax Credit. The primary federal program for financing affordable housing construction. Many mixed-income buildings are LIHTC-financed.
Cross-subsidy
The financial mechanism by which revenue from market-rate units in a mixed-income building offsets the revenue shortfall from below-market-rate units.
Density bonus
Permission granted to a developer to build more units than baseline zoning allows, in exchange for including affordable units.
HUD
U.S. Department of Housing and Urban Development. The federal agency overseeing housing policy, fair housing enforcement, and housing finance regulation.
FMR / SAFMR
Fair Market Rent / Small Area Fair Market Rent. HUD's annual estimate of the 40th percentile of asking rents for standard units in a given area, calculated at the zip code level.
ZORI
Zillow Observed Rent Index. Zillow's monthly index of asking rents across all home types, calculated from active listings data.
Filtering
The economic process by which housing units become more affordable over time as they age, depreciate, and are vacated by higher-income residents moving into newer units.
YIMBY
"Yes In My Backyard." A housing advocacy movement that supports new residential construction on the grounds that increased supply reduces rents broadly through filtering and demand absorption.
NIMBY
"Not In My Backyard." Opposition to new development by existing residents concerned about neighborhood character, parking, traffic, or density.
TOD
Transit-Oriented Development. A planning strategy concentrating higher-density mixed-use development within walking distance of transit stations. Often paired with IZ policies.
Diff-in-diff
Difference-in-differences. A statistical research methodology comparing outcomes before/after a policy change in treated areas vs. untreated control areas.
Demand absorption
The mechanism by which new housing units capture high-income renters who would otherwise compete for existing lower-cost units, relieving pressure on older and more affordable stock.
Induced demand
The phenomenon where new supply creates its own new demand — new housing attracts new residents rather than simply accommodating existing demand.
